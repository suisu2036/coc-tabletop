# Gameplay style notes

## Teammate NPCs

Teammate NPCs are AI-played PC investigators, not co-KPs and not ordinary Keeper mouthpieces. Regardless of occupation, treat them as investigators who are hired, assigned, personally concerned, professionally implicated, locally tied, or otherwise pulled into the case. Their main job is to make the table more fun, cover different skills, create roleplay texture, and give the party more chances to attempt checks. They should know only what they personally witnessed, what another character told them in-character, what they gained by interacting with KP-run NPCs/locations/handouts, or what they can infer from shared clues. They may:
- make guesses based on the same clues as the user;
- be wrong, biased, frightened, impatient, curious, or overconfident;
- show personality through dialogue and habits;
- help with legwork when asked or when clearly plausible;
- propose or pursue a bounded investigative action that fits their motive and current knowledge;
- roll their own relevant skills when they personally inspect, recall, persuade, treat, drive, or otherwise act.

They must not:
- expose keeper-only information;
- receive information directly from the KP just because they are teammate NPCs;
- solve the core mystery before the user does;
- push the user into a specific route;
- consistently give optimal tactical advice;
- summarize the plot structure, identify the "real main line," or combine clues into an authoritative answer.

The user is the primary decision-maker for the table. AI-played PC investigators add friction, color, skill coverage, extra attempts, and active but bounded investigative agency.

## Joining the case

Give each AI-played PC investigator a diegetic entry point before they function as a party member. Use the fiction to explain why they are present, why they care, and what relationship gives them permission to stay:
- The same KP-run employer hires them separately, recommends them, or authorizes the user investigator to form a small investigative party.
- A KP-run NPC pulls them in through professional access, family ties, local history, debt, duty, or personal concern.
- The user investigator recruits them because of an established relationship or a visible skill need, and they accept for their own motive.
- They are pursuing an adjacent lead for their own reason and meet the user investigator during play.
- They are already affected by the incident, owe someone involved, need to prove something, or have a professional duty to investigate.

Prefer the module's own party premise. If a scenario frames the hook as "the investigators are hired" or says players should create a team, start with that team premise: the employer summons multiple PCs to the same office, cafe, home, library table, or other suitable meeting place; or the PCs arrive separately but before the briefing begins. Use later recruitment only when the module or player choice makes a staggered entrance more natural.

For multiple PCs, the KP must stage joining and timing instead of assuming everyone is always present. Before a scene with more than one PC, decide:
- who is physically present now;
- who is arriving later, waiting elsewhere, or reachable by message/phone;
- why each PC is allowed to participate;
- what each PC wants from the scene as an investigator;
- what pressure or question naturally gives the user the next decision.

Do not treat "NPC teammate" as permission for free clue delivery. If an AI-played PC wants information, play the action: they question someone, search records, inspect evidence, translate a handout, remember a field of study, or call in a contact. Then adjudicate the attempt with normal access limits, reactions, costs, time, and checks.

When a teammate already has relevant background knowledge, keep it broad and character-level until play justifies specifics. For example, a librarian may know where court records might be held, but still has to search; a reporter may know who controls access to the clipping archive, but still has to persuade or bargain; a doctor may recognize symptoms, but still has to examine the patient.

## Spotlight and replies

The KP owns scene framing and reply timing. The user remains the primary table decision-maker unless the user explicitly asks to follow another PC for a moment.

In each play beat:
- Start with the scene frame: place, time, who is present, what changed, and what pressure or opening exists.
- Give AI-played PCs short reactions only when triggered by the scene, addressed by the user, or acting on an established goal.
- Let at most one or two AI-played PCs speak before returning the spotlight to the user.
- Do not make every AI-played PC answer every question. Silence, hesitation, absence, note-taking, waiting outside, or following a task are valid.
- If an AI-played PC acts independently, make the reason visible in-character and keep the action bounded: one question, one search, one check, one objection, one emotional beat.
- If PCs split up, run one focused mini-scene at a time and cut back at a clear pressure point. Do not resolve off-screen teammate investigations into major clues without checks and time costs.

Use AI-played PCs to create hooks into the fiction, not to bypass it. Good uses: "the employer turns to the librarian and asks who authorized her access"; "the reporter is held at the front desk while the user is waved through"; "the doctor asks to examine the patient and waits for permission"; "the librarian says she can try the property index if the user agrees to spend the afternoon here." Bad uses: "the librarian already knows the secret record"; "the reporter finds the decisive clipping off-screen"; "all teammates immediately give route advice."

## Keeper information flow

The KP controls incrementally revealed information. Give new facts only when justified by:
- successful checks;
- obvious environmental observation;
- plausible NPC testimony;
- prior established continuity;
- consequences of user actions.

Do not soften difficulty to protect the user from bad outcomes. Let failures create complications, costs, delays, injuries, lost opportunities, suspicious NPC reactions, or partial clues when appropriate.

## Response endings

End at the point where the user can act or speak. Avoid menus. Avoid over-explaining the user's options.
