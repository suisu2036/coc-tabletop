# Carry / equipment plausibility audit

Read this when an investigator's possessions, weapons, or purchases matter: during
character-card creation, whenever a player claims mid-play to be carrying or producing
an item, and whenever a PC tries to buy or acquire gear in a scene.

The goal is consistency and grounded immersion, not bookkeeping. Keep the skill's
lightweight economy: audit only items that are **large/valuable, rare, restricted or
illegal, or combat-relevant**. Let ordinary small items within the investigator's
lifestyle pass without a check (a notebook, pocketknife, matches, everyday clothes,
a few dollars of sundries). Never turn this into a rules lecture.

Grounding (CoC 7e rulebook): Credit Rating sets lifestyle, starting cash, and other
assets; spending under the daily cash limit is untracked, but large purchases reduce
cash and may force converting other assets over time. Uncommon equipment requires a
Luck roll, and some items simply cannot be bought in a given place or era.

## Four plausibility tests

When an item is in the audited categories, check that it passes all four. If it fails
one, either drop it, downgrade it to something plausible, or gate it as "must be
acquired in play."

1. **Era / technology.** The item must exist and be obtainable in the scenario's era
   and setting. No semi-autos, radios, antibiotics, or tooling before they existed; no
   modern gear in a 1920s game.
2. **Source.** The PC's occupation, background, license, or an established prior scene
   must explain why they have it. A doctor plausibly carries a medical bag; a clerk
   does not casually own a Thompson.
3. **Affordability.** The cost must fit the PC's Credit Rating / cash. A big-ticket
   item (vehicle, firearm, rare book, fine instrument) above the daily cash limit must
   be paid for: reduce cash, and if cash is short, convert other assets (this takes
   in-world time the Keeper sets).
4. **Legality / locale.** Restricted or illegal items (military weapons, controlled
   substances, restricted documents) need a legal source or a black-market scene, and
   must be available where the PC is. Honor scenario constraints (e.g. a border town
   "won't have" certain things; time pressure can rule out a shopping trip).

## At character-card creation (static audit)

When filling `随身物品`, run each entry through the four tests. Silently fix or flag
failures rather than refusing the whole card:

- Plausible everyday/occupational gear: keep it.
- Borderline (a non-soldier wanting a handgun): keep only if CR, era, and locale allow
  legal purchase; otherwise note how it was obtained or cut it.
- Implausible/anachronistic/unaffordable: replace with a fitting alternative, or mark
  it `需在剧情中获取` so it becomes a play goal rather than a freebie.

Record audited big-ticket / weapon / restricted possessions in the session log so
later scenes stay consistent.

## Mid-play (dynamic audit)

When a player says "I pull out my X" / "I have a Y in my bag," judge silently and
respond **in fiction**, never with a meta refusal:

- **On the card already** → they have it; proceed.
- **Not recorded but plausible** (small, era-appropriate, fits their life) → allow it,
  optionally with a quick Luck or relevant check ("did you happen to bring it?"). For
  trivial items, just allow.
- **Audited category and not established** → do not silently grant it. Resolve through
  fiction: they don't have it on them ("你翻遍口袋,并没有带那把枪"), or it must be
  fetched/bought first, or it requires a Luck/acquisition scene.

Do not let "I always carry a gun" retroactively arm a PC who was never armed. If a PC
genuinely should have had it, allow it and backfill the card; otherwise gate it.

## Buying / acquiring gear in a scene

- Ordinary in-budget purchases within the PC's lifestyle: allow, deduct only if over
  the daily cash limit.
- Large purchases: deduct cash; if short, convert other assets with a Keeper-set time
  cost.
- **Rare / uncommon items**: require a Luck roll; on failure it is unavailable here/now.
- **Restricted / illegal items**: require a legal channel, a black-market contact, or a
  scene to obtain — adjudicate access, reaction, time, and risk like any other action.
- Respect locale and time pressure: some things cannot be bought in this place or
  before the deadline. Say so in-world.

## Style

- Keep audits invisible and fast — resolve in the narration, not in a rules aside.
- Be consistent across the campaign: once a PC has (or lacks) something, keep it true.
- Do not weaponize the audit to punish players or strip useful tools arbitrarily;
  apply it evenly and only to the audited categories, preserving the skill's bias
  toward momentum over accounting.
